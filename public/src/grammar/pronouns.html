Pronouns

Orcish is a language that implies many of its pronouns. This is because the person and number of Orcish verbs, and the case of Orcish nouns and adjectives, can be determined by the endings of those words. This alleviates much of the need for pronouns that other languages face. This is not to say that Orcish does not have or does not use pronouns—it does, but sparingly, and usually only when the existing words in a sentence are not enough to convey a complete meaning. A reflexive “–self” pronoun can be created by attaching the suffix –kel to the end of the pronoun, regardless of case. Thus, “I hurt myself” is fonag nudzkel (nudzkel [myself] = nudz + –kel).

1st Person Pronouns
Singular Plural
Nominative: na (I) noba (we)
Genitive: nur (of me) nobur (of us)
Dative: no (me [indir. obj.]) nobo (us [indir. obj.])
Accusative: nudz (me [dir. obj.]) nubudz (us [dir. obj.])
Vocative: no (O, I) nobo (O, we)
2nd Person Pronouns
Singular Plural
Nominative: dra (you) draba (you all)
Genitive: drur (of you) drabur (of you all)
Dative: dor (you [indir. obj.]) drobo (you all [indir. obj.])
Accusative: dru (you [dir. obj.]) drobudz (you all [dir. obj.])
Vocative: dor (O, you) drobo (O, you all)
3rd Person Pronouns (Singular)
Masculine Feminine Neutral
Nominative: mul (he) pad (she) od (it)
Genitive: mu (of he) par (of she) u (of it)
Dative: mo (him [indir.]) pae (her [indir.]) u (it [indir.])
Accusative: mudz (him [dir.]) paz (her [dir.]) odz (it [dir.])

3rd Person Pronouns (Plural)
Masculine Feminine Neutral
Nominative: mulz (they) padz (they) idz (they)
Genitive: murru (of them) parru (of them) urru (of them)
Dative: mors (them [indir.]) paes (them [indir.]) od (them [indir.])
Accusative: muluz (them [dir.]) paruz (them [dir.]) uluz (them [dir.])
Vocative: mors (O, they) paes (O, they) od (O, they)
Notes:
  1. Unlike in English, the personal pronoun “I” (na) is not capitalized.
  2. The genitive pronouns are rarely used in modern conversation; most orcs prefer to use possessive pronouns to indicate possession. Use of the genitive pronoun case is relegated mostly to ceremonial speech. It is included here for the sake of completion.
  3. For the 3rd person plural, when the genders of the individuals are mixed, unknown, or entirely male, the masculine pronoun (mulz) is used; when the individuals are entirely female, the feminine pronoun (padz) is used; when the pronoun is referencing objects, the neutral form (idz) is used. These pronouns decline normally.
  4. indir. obj = indirect object; dir. obj. = direct object
  5. indir. = indirect object; dir. = direct object

Possessive pronouns indicate possession. They are much more frequently used in the Orcish lexicon than the genitive case of the personal pronouns.

The demonstrative pronouns/adjectives “this” and “that” and their plurals are used to describe an object close at hand (this/these) or something observed by the speaker (that/those). Both sets (this/these and that/those) exist in the 3rd person. They take the appropriate gender of the noun they are describing.

Notes:
  1. As with personal pronouns, genitive case reflexive pronouns are rarely used and are presented here for the sake of completion.
  2. For plural pronouns, when the genders of the objects are mixed, or unknown, or entirely male, the masculine pronoun (ulz or lulz) is used; when the objects are entirely female, the feminine pronoun (adz or ladz) is used; when the objects are entirely neutral in gender, the neutral pronoun (idz or lidz) is used. These pronouns decline as normal.

The relative and interrogative pronouns “who” and “which” are used 1) to indicate nouns previously mentioned, such as in: “I ravaged the town, which was very rich” or “The thief who stole my pants”; or 2) in questions such as “With which atrocity-knife shall I remove your heart?” or “Who stole my pants?”. These pronouns exist in the 3rd person and take the appropriate gender of the noun they are referencing.
