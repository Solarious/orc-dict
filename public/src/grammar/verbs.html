Verbs and Conjugation

The endings of Orcish verbs communicate the number and person performing the verb’s action. There are two numbers (singular and plural) and three persons (1st, 2nd, and 3rd), resulting in six total combinations. They are:

Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: I we
2nd: you you all
3rd: he/she/it they

Verbs in this dictionary are listed in their infinitive forms. Verbs that end in –a are 1st Conjugation verbs; those ending in –ai are 2nd Conjugation verbs, and follow a different pattern presented later in this section. All 1st Conjugation verbs in their infinitive forms end with the suffix –a, as in ka, (to kill), pekka (to defeat), and viska, (to gut). Attach the proper ending to the infinitive form of a verb to indicate person and number. Below are the 1st Conjugation endings for the Present Active voice.

1st Conj. Present Active
Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: –g –gax
2nd: –s –gas
3rd: –k –gek

Thus, kag means “I kill” (ka + –g, 1st person singular) and pekkagek means “they defeat” (pekka + –gek, 3rd person plural).

The Past Active and Future Active voices are indicated by adding a prefix to the conjugated verb: ash– for Past Active and ar– for Future Active. Thus, ashdobnagax means “we worked” (ash– + dobna (to work) + –gax) and arvansas translates into “you will understand” (ar– + vasna (to understand) + –s).

The passive voice is used when the performer of an action in a sentence is unknown, or where the object of the sentence is also the subject. It is the difference between “I love” (dovag) and “I am loved” (dovareg). All 1st Conjugation Orcish verbs in the passive infinitive end in –are, such as dovare (“to be loved”). Below are the 1st Conjugation endings for the Present Passive voice.

1st Conj. Present Passive
Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: –reg –regax
2nd: –ras –regas
3rd: –ruk –regek

As with their active voice counterparts, the 1st Conjugation Past Passive and Future Passive voices are indicated with the attachment of the same respective prefix: ash– for Past Passive, ar– for Future Passive. Thus, ashgadzaregax translates into “we were stopped” (ash– + gadza [to stop] + –regax) and arshaggaregas means “you all will be slain” (ar– + shagga [to slay] + –regas).

The 1st Conjugation Past Perfect voice, also known as the Pluperfect, is used to indicate action that was completed at some point in the past. The Past Perfect is indicated by attaching the prefix hush– to the verb along with the appropriate ending. For example, “I killed the dwarves” (Past Active) in Orcish would be ashkag duwuluz, but “I have killed the dwarves” (Past Perfect) would be hushkag duwuluz (hushkag = hush– + ka + –g).

Notes:
  1. A holdover from Middle Orcish that is still perfectly acceptable is the original Past Perfect prefix, huash–. It is still used in place of the shorter and more widely accepted hush– by some of the clans who settled closest to the Underkingdom after the Red Exodus.
  2. Many orcs will use huash– in place of hush– in formal language or on ceremonial oc- casions. Use of huash– otherwise, especially for outsiders and non-orcs, is considered old-fashioned at best, and pompous and condescending at worst.
  3. huash– is implemented on this website as a prefix

The 1st Conjugation Future Perfect voice is used to indicate action that will be completed at some point in the future, and is indicated by attaching the prefix hur– to the verb along with an appropriate ending. Thus, “We will kill the dwarves” (Future Active) in Orcish would be arkagax duwuluz, but “We will have killed the dwarves” (Future Perfect) would be hurkagax duwuluz (hurkag = hur– + ka + –gax).

  1. As with its Past Perfect counterpart, the Future Perfect also has an archaic alternate prefix that is still widely used: huar–.
  2. Use of huar– rather than hur– is similarly used mainly in formal settings, and use of it outside such occasions is seen as silly or pretentious.
  3. huar– is implemented on this website as a prefix

The 1st Conjugation Past Perfect Passive and Future Perfect Passive voices follow the same basic pattern as their active voice equivalents: a prefix (hush– for the Past Perfect Passive, hur– for the Future Perfect Passive), the verb, and an ending combine to describe action that was completed in the past, or that will be completed in the future, in which the agent is hidden or missing, or when the subject is also the object. Thus, “He has been eaten” translates into Orcish as hushpregnaruk (hush– + pregna [to eat]+ –ruk) and “They will have been destroyed” becomes hurtararegek (hur– + tara [to destroy] + –regek).

The 1st Conjugation Imperative Mood is used to express commands and requests. As moods go, it is remarkably simple. To express an imperative command, attach the appropriate ending to the infinitive form of the verb. The main determining factor is the number of the verb, whether the command or request is being issued to a single person or a group. For example, the command “Kill the dwarf” (which has an implied “you” at the beginning of the sentence, as in “You kill the dwarf”), given to a single person, would be kart duwudz; the same command given to a group of people (implied: “[You all] kill the dwarf”) would be karit duwudz.

Number: Singular Plural
Person: 2nd: –rt –rit

2nd Conjugation Orcish verbs in their infinitive forms end with the suffix –ai, as in hai (to aid), sakai (to dispute), and gurkai (to harm). 2nd Conjugation verbs follow a different pattern when they conjugate. To conjugate a 2nd Conjugation verb, first remove the infinitive suffix (–ai) and then attach the proper ending. Below are the 2nd Conjugation endings for the Present Active voice.

2nd Conj. Present Active
Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: –ai –alax
2nd: –ash –ahas
3rd: –ak –ahek

Thus, hai means “I aid” (h[–ai] + –ai, 1st person singular) and gurkahek means “they harm” (gurk[–ai] + –ahek, 3rd person plural).

Notes:
  1. You will note that the 1st person singular (–ai) uses the exact same ending as the 2nd Conjugation infinitive (–ai). This can be a source of confusion. If it is unclear which version is intended, study the sentence structure; conjugated verbs typically come near the beginning of an Orcish sentence, with verbs in their infinitive form at the end.
  2. The 3rd person singular (–ak) closely resembles the 3rd person singular of the 1st Conjugation (–k). Given how closely some 1st and 2nd Conjugation verbs resemble one another, you may have to rely on context to convey proper meaning; without it, there’s no way to tell the difference between djonak nodz (she rents me a room; 2nd Conj. verb djonai) and djonak nodz (she shoves me in a bag; 1st Conj. verb djona)

As with their 1st Conjucation counterparts, 2nd Conjugation Past Active and Future Active voices are indicated by adding a prefix to the conjugated verb: zsa– for Past Active and zsur– for Future Active. As with all 2nd Conjugation verbs, the –ai suffix must be shed before attaching the new ending. Thus, zsafukkash means “you competed” (zsa– + fukk[–ai] [to compete] + –ash) and zsurkrumbalax translates into “we will ruin” (zsur– + krumbl[–ai] [to ruin] + –alax).

The passive voice is used when the performer of an action is unknown or intentionally obscured, or when an object is the subject of a sentence. As with the rest of the 2nd Conjugation, the infinitive suffix is shed before attaching the appropriate ending. Thus, gadzaek means “he is denied” (gadz[–ai] [to deny] + –aek) and tarlalax mean “we are adopted” (tarl[– ai] [to adopt] + alax). 2nd Conjugation Orcish verbs in the passive infinitive end in –ae. Thus, hae means “to be aided” (h[–ai] [to aid] + –ae) and yossae translates into “to be spoiled” (yoss[–ai] [to spoil] + –ae). Below are the 2st Conjugation endings for the Present Passive voice.

The 2nd Conjugation Past Perfect voice, also known as the Pluperfect, is used to indicate action that was completed at some point in the past. The Past Perfect is indicated by attaching the prefix huzs– to the verb along with the appropriate ending (after the infinitive –ai is shed). For example, “I blinded the elves” (Past Active) would be zsakultai helaruz, but “I have blinded the elves” (Past Perfect) would be huzskultai helaruz (huzskultai = huzs– + kult[–ai] + –ai).

The 2nd Conjugation Past Perfect Passive and Future Perfect Passive voices follow the same basic pattern as their active voice equivalents: a prefix (huzs– for the Past Perfect Passive, azsur– for the Future Perfect Passive), the verb, and an ending (once the infinitive suffix has been shed) combine to describe action that was completed in the past, or that will be completed in the future, in which the agent is hidden or missing, or when the subject is also the object. Thus, “You have been rehabilitated” translates into Orcish as huzsnomortesh (huzs– + nomort[–ai] [to rehabilitate] + –esh) and “They will have been herded together” becomes azsurlidbarrak (azsur– + lid[–ai] [to herd together] + –barrak).

The 2nd Conjugation Imperative Mood is used to express commands and requests. As with its 1st Conjugation counterpart, it is remarkably simple. To express an a 2nd Conjugation imperative, remove the –ai suffix from the infinitive, and then attach the appropriate ending. Again, the determining factor is the number of the verb: whether the command is being issued to a single person or a group. For example, the command “Cut the elf in half” (which has an implied “you” at the beginning of the sentence), given to an individual, would be numort helaz (numai = to halve); the same command given to a group of people (implied: “[You all] cut the elf in half”) would be nomorot helax.

Person: 2nd: –ort –orot

The Irregular Verb “To Be” (infinitive = aa) is one of the language’s only irregular verbs. In most languages, the verb “to be” is irregular, and even a language as rigidly structured as Orcish is no exception. It is presented below in its most common forms: the Present, Past, and Future Active voices. The future infinitive form of aa is araa: “there will be.”

Present Active
Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: aga (I am) aggax (we are)
2nd: aas (you am) aggas (you all are)
3rd: aka (he/she/it is; this/that is) aggek (they are; these/those are)
Past Active
Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: ashag (I was) ashax (we were)
2nd: ashas (you were) ashagas (you all were)
3rd: ashek (he/she/it was; this/that was) ashagek (there were; these/those were)
Future Active
Number: Singular Plural
Person: 1st: areg (I will be) areggax (we will be)
2nd: aras (you will be) areggas (you all will be)
3rd: arek (he/she/it will be; this/that will be) araggek (there will be; these/those will be)

Modal verbs—verbs such as “can” (matha), “may” (possa), and “shall” (grega)—conjugate as normal, and the verbs they assist take the infinitive and move to the end of the sentence. Thus, “Can you skin that dwarf?” is translated Mathas duwudz haeda? (haeda = to skin), and “We shall burn the dwarves.” becomes Gregagax duwuluz trogda (trogda = to burn).
